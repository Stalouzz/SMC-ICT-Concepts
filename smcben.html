Core Definitions

Every concept below has two explanations: Simple language for intuition, then technical language for precision. Read both — together they build real understanding.

Sell-Side Liquidity SSL

Simple: Imagine every trader who bought and placed their stop-loss below a recent swing low. All those stop orders pile up under that low like a cluster of landmines. The area below swing lows is called sell-side liquidity — it's where stops from long traders sit, waiting to be triggered.

Technical: Sell-side liquidity refers to the concentration of resting sell orders — specifically stop-loss orders from long positions and breakout sell orders from short-traders — that accumulate below identifiable swing lows, equal lows, trendline lows, and round number support levels. When price reaches this zone, these orders execute as market sells, creating a burst of downward momentum.
Buy-Side Liquidity BSL

Simple: Now flip it. Every short seller placed their stop-loss above a swing high. Every breakout trader placed a buy order just above a resistance level. All of those pile up above swing highs — that's buy-side liquidity. The area above highs is where stops from short trades sit.

Technical: Buy-side liquidity is the concentration of resting buy orders — stop-losses from short positions and breakout buy orders from long traders — that accumulate above swing highs, equal highs, trendline highs, and round number resistance levels. When price reaches this zone, these orders execute as market buys, producing upward momentum.
Liquidity Sweep Sweep

Simple: A liquidity sweep is when price briefly spikes into a cluster of stops, triggers them all, then immediately turns around and moves the other way. Think of it as a "fake breakout" engineered to collect orders before the real move.

Technical: A liquidity sweep (or "stop hunt") occurs when price extends beyond a key level — a swing high or low — triggering resting orders in that zone, then rapidly reverses. The sweep fills institutional order needs at better prices. Confirmation typically appears as a wick beyond the level + strong close back inside the range, often with a simultaneous shift in market structure.
Invalidation Risk Mgmt

Simple: Invalidation is the exact point on the chart where your trade idea is proven wrong. If price reaches that point, the reason you entered no longer exists. Your stop-loss should be placed there.

Technical: Invalidation is the price level at which the conditions that justified a trade entry are structurally negated. For a bullish setup, invalidation occurs when price breaks and closes below the origin point of the bullish order block or below the most recent higher low. For a bearish setup, invalidation is a close above the bearish order block or above the most recent lower high.
Order Block OB

Simple: An order block is the last candle (or small group of candles) moving in the opposite direction before a strong, explosive move. Institutions often left unfilled orders there, and price tends to return to that zone before resuming the trend.

Technical: An order block is typically identified as the last bearish candle before a bullish impulse (bullish OB) or the last bullish candle before a bearish impulse (bearish OB). It represents a zone where large institutional orders were placed. When price retraces into this zone, remaining orders are filled, often producing a reaction. A valid OB is one that caused a break of structure or CHoCH on its initial move.
Break of Structure BOS

Simple: In an uptrend, price makes higher highs. A Break of Structure happens when price takes out (closes above) the most recent swing high. It's trend confirmation — the uptrend is continuing.

Technical: A BOS occurs when price closes beyond the most recent swing point in the direction of the prevailing trend. In a bullish trend: price closes above the last higher high. In a bearish trend: price closes below the last lower low. BOS signals trend continuation. It is used to confirm bias, not to enter trades — entries follow pullbacks after BOS.
Change of Character CHoCH

Simple: CHoCH is the first sign the trend might be turning. In a downtrend, price has been making lower highs and lower lows. A CHoCH happens when price suddenly breaks above a previous lower high for the first time. That's a warning sign — something has changed.

Technical: A CHoCH is the first break against the prevailing trend structure. In a downtrend: the first close above a lower high. In an uptrend: the first close below a higher low. CHoCH does not confirm reversal — it signals potential reversal. Traders wait for subsequent BOS confirmation and look for a pullback into an order block or imbalance before entering.
Premium & Discount PD Array

Simple: Think of price like a sale. When price is low relative to its recent range — it's on discount (good to buy). When price is high relative to its range — it's at a premium (good to sell). Institutions buy at discount, sell at premium.

Technical: Using the 50% equilibrium (EQ) of a defined price range (swing high to swing low): prices trading above 50% are in premium — ideal for selling short. Prices trading below 50% are in discount — ideal for buying long. Entries taken at extreme premium (75–100% of range) or extreme discount (0–25% of range) offer the best risk/reward ratios when combined with an order block and structural confirmation.

Market Structure

Market structure is the skeleton of price action. Before anything else, you must identify what the market is doing on the timeframe you're trading — and the timeframes above it.

The Three Market States

BULLISH STRUCTURE Higher Highs (HH) + Higher Lows (HL) Price → makes new HH → pulls back to HL → pushes to new HH again Pattern: HL → HH → HL → HH → HL → HH BEARISH STRUCTURE Lower Highs (LH) + Lower Lows (LL) Price → makes new LL → pulls back to LH → drops to new LL Pattern: LH → LL → LH → LL → LH → LL RANGING / CONSOLIDATION No clear direction Price bounces between defined high and low — equal highs / equal lows Often precedes a sweep of one side before a directional move

BOS vs CHoCH — Know the Difference

Signal What it Breaks What it Means Action
BOS Breaks WITH the trend (HH in uptrend, LL in downtrend) Trend continuation confirmed Wait for pullback to OB/discount for entry
CHoCH Breaks AGAINST the trend (breaks a LH in downtrend) Possible reversal beginning Watch for confirmation, wait for OB pullback
DOWNTREND REVERSAL SEQUENCE: LH → LL → LH → LL → LH → CHoCH (breaks LH) → pullback → BOS (breaks HH) → new uptrend The CHoCH gets your attention. The BOS confirms the turn. The OB pullback gives the entry.

Multi-Timeframe Alignment

Structure on a higher timeframe (HTF) overrules everything below it. Always align your trade with the HTF bias.

  • HTF (Daily/4H): Defines the dominant trend and major liquidity pools
  • MTF (1H/15M): Confirms structure shift, identifies the order block to trade from
  • LTF (5M/1M): Finds precise entry within the OB, confirms CHoCH on entry timeframe
03

Liquidity — Where Stops Live

Liquidity is the fuel price needs to move. Without a pool of orders to fill, large participants cannot execute their trades without moving the market against themselves. Understanding where retail traders place their stops is understanding where price is likely to go next.

Where Does Liquidity Form?

Liquidity Type Location on Chart Who Created It
Sell-Side (SSL) Below swing lows, below equal lows, below trendlines Stop-losses of long traders; breakout shorts
Buy-Side (BSL) Above swing highs, above equal highs, above trendlines Stop-losses of short traders; breakout longs
Round Numbers 1.3000, 1.2500, 150.00 — psychological levels Retail orders clustered at obvious numbers
Range Extremes Above/below consolidation zones Breakout traders on both sides

Why Price Raids Liquidity Before Moving

Large institutional traders need the opposite side of the market to fill their orders. If an institution wants to buy millions of units, it needs sellers. The most sellers exist at stop-loss clusters below swing lows (sell-side liquidity). So price is pushed down into that zone, triggers all the stops (which become market sell orders), and the institution buys from those sellers at the low. Then price reverses.

⚡
Key insight: The liquidity sweep is not random. Price does not "accidentally" spike below a low. It is engineered to collect orders. The spike is the move; what comes after the spike is the trade.
LIQUIDITY GRAB SEQUENCE (Bullish): Price approaches SSL below swing low → Price breaks below swing low (triggers stops / fills sell orders) → Price immediately wicks back above the low (rejection wick) → CHoCH forms on lower timeframe → Price retraces into bullish order block → Long entry, targeting BSL above LIQUIDITY GRAB SEQUENCE (Bearish): Price approaches BSL above swing high → Price breaks above swing high (triggers stops) → Price immediately wicks back below (rejection wick) → CHoCH forms on lower timeframe → Price retraces into bearish order block → Short entry, targeting SSL below
04

Order Blocks

How to Identify a Valid Order Block

Rule of thumb: An order block is valid only if the candle (or zone) caused a move that resulted in a Break of Structure or a Change of Character. If the impulse after it was weak and sideways, it is not a reliable OB.

Bullish Order Block Identification

  • Find a strong bullish impulse move that broke structure (BOS) or caused a CHoCH
  • Identify the last bearish candle directly before that impulse began
  • That candle's body (open to close) + its high forms the bullish OB zone
  • When price returns to that zone, look for bullish reaction (long entry area)

Bearish Order Block Identification

  • Find a strong bearish impulse move that broke structure or caused a CHoCH
  • Identify the last bullish candle directly before that impulse began
  • That candle's body (open to close) + its low forms the bearish OB zone
  • When price returns to that zone, look for bearish reaction (short entry area)

Why Order Blocks Matter

Order blocks represent areas where institutional traders placed large orders. Not all of those orders were filled when price first moved through. When price returns (retraces), the remaining unfilled orders get triggered, causing price to react. This is why OBs act as support and resistance — not because of a line on a chart, but because of actual resting order flow.

Breaker Blocks

Breaker Block

Simple: When an order block fails — price breaks through it and continues — it "flips" and often becomes resistance (if it was support) or support (if it was resistance).

Technical: A bullish order block that is violated by a close below it becomes a bearish breaker — price will often return to it and sell from there. A bearish OB violated by a close above becomes a bullish breaker. Breakers are useful for counter-trend entries after a significant structure shift.

Full Trade Logic Framework

This is the complete decision tree for building a trade from scratch. Every step matters. Skipping steps leads to errors.

The 7-Step Trade Build

STEP 1
HTF Bias
Determine the higher timeframe (HTF) directional bias. Is the Daily/4H chart in a bullish or bearish market structure? All trades should align with this bias.
STEP 2
Liquidity
Identify where the nearest liquidity pools are on the HTF. Where are the buy-side pools (above highs)? Where are the sell-side pools (below lows)? Price is likely heading toward one of them.
STEP 3
Sweep Watch
Watch for a liquidity sweep at a key level. Did price spike below a swing low and come right back? Or spike above a high and immediately reject? This is your setup trigger.
STEP 4
CHoCH / BOS
After the sweep, wait for a Change of Character on the lower timeframe (LTF). This is the first structural break against the sweep direction — confirmation the reversal is beginning.
STEP 5
Order Block
After the CHoCH, price will often retrace. Identify the order block that formed during the initial impulse (the candle before the CHoCH move). This is your entry zone.
STEP 6
Entry
Enter when price returns to the OB zone. Entry can be at the OB's level (limit order) or on a lower-timeframe confirmation candle within the OB. Enter in discount for longs; in premium for shorts.
STEP 7
SL / TP
Place stop-loss below (for longs) or above (for shorts) the OB zone — usually a few pips beyond the sweep wick's extreme. Set take-profit at the next liquidity pool (opposing side BSL or SSL).

Entry, Stop-Loss, and Take-Profit Logic

ComponentBullish SetupBearish Setup
Entry Return to bullish OB (in discount, below 50% of range) Return to bearish OB (in premium, above 50% of range)
Stop Loss A few pips below the sweep low / bottom of OB A few pips above the sweep high / top of OB
Take Profit 1 Most recent swing high (equal highs / buy-side liquidity) Most recent swing low (equal lows / sell-side liquidity)
Take Profit 2 HTF swing high / major buy-side pool HTF swing low / major sell-side pool
Invalidation Price closes below the sweep low (OB violated) Price closes above the sweep high (OB violated)
Risk-to-reward note: With this framework, your stop is tight (just beyond the OB / sweep) and your target is a full liquidity pool. This typically produces setups with 3:1 to 5:1 or higher RR when executed correctly. Protect capital first — always define risk before entry.

Bullish Trade Example

Pair: EUR/USD | Timeframes: 4H bias, 1H entry, 15M confirmation

🟢 Bullish Setup — Liquidity Sweep + OB Retest EUR/USD · 1H Chart
BSL ──────────────────────────────────────── 1.0920 ← TARGET (equal highs above) ↑ BOS ┌──┐ impulse move up ┌─┘ └────────────── │ Bullish OB zone ─────── │ [OB: 1.0862–1.0875] ← ENTRY ZONE (last bearish candle │ before impulse) │ ← CHoCH formed here (first break of LH) │ ┐ │ │ swing low ↓ SSL ─────────└──┐ sweep wick ──── 1.0848 ← STOP BELOW HERE (1.0843) └ (wick below, then closes back up)
①
HTF Bias (4H): EUR/USD is in bullish market structure — making HH and HL. The daily chart shows price above the 50% equilibrium of a recent major leg up. Bias is bullish.
②
Liquidity Pool Identified: Below the previous swing low at 1.0848, there is sell-side liquidity — stops from longs who entered at that low. Above at 1.0920 sit equal highs — buy-side liquidity is the target.
③
Sweep Occurs: Price drops below 1.0848, sweeping sell-side liquidity. A long wick appears on the 1H candle — but the candle closes back above 1.0848. This is the sweep signal.
④
CHoCH on 15M: On the 15M chart, price breaks above the most recent lower high formed during the drop — this is the CHoCH. Upside momentum is beginning.
⑤
OB Identified: The last bearish 15M candle before the CHoCH impulse sits at 1.0862–1.0875. This is the bullish order block. Price is expected to retrace into this zone.
⑥
Entry & Levels: Limit buy placed at 1.0868 (inside the OB). Stop-loss set at 1.0843 (below the sweep wick extreme). Take profit targeting 1.0920 (BSL at equal highs). RR ≈ 3.0:1.

Psychology Behind This Move

Retail Long
Entered long at the swing low (1.0848), placed stop just below at 1.0844. Feels confident — it's a "support level."
Retail Short
Sees price breaking below support and enters short at 1.0847 with stop above 1.0855. Thinks it's a breakout.
Institution
Pushes price below 1.0848 to trigger all those stops (sell orders flood the market). Buys the liquidity from both retail longs' stops AND retail shorts' entries at the best possible price.
Result
Retail longs stopped out at a loss. Retail shorts trapped in a losing long move. Institution accumulates a massive long position at the sweep low, then drives price to BSL above.

②

⑤
Bearish OB Located: The last bullish 1H candle before the CHoCH impulse down is at 1.2745–1.2760. This is the bearish order block. Price will likely retrace up into this zone.
⑥
Entry & Levels: Limit sell placed at 1.2752 (inside the bearish OB). Stop-loss at 1.2785 (above the sweep wick). Take profit targeting 1.2650 (SSL at swing lows). RR ≈ 3.1:1.

Fakeout / Liquidity Sweep Example

Pair: GBP/JPY | Timeframes: 4H bias, 1H chart | Scenario: Range environment with sweep of equal lows

⚡ Fakeout — Equal Lows Sweep + Bullish Reversal GBP/JPY · 1H Chart
Range High / BSL ─────────────────── 192.80 ← TARGET ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ CONSOLIDATION RANGE (price ranging, equal highs & lows) │ │ price bounces between 191.00 and 192.80 │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Equal Lows (SSL) ──────────────────── 191.00 Equal Lows (SSL) ──────────────────── 191.00 ↓ SWEEP ┌── 190.72 (wick low) └ (candle closes BACK above 191.00) OB Zone ─────────────────── [191.00–191.25] ← ENTRY ↑ CHoCH on 15M ┌───────────────────── 192.80 STOP: 190.60 (below wick extreme)
①
Range Identified: GBP/JPY has been consolidating between 191.00 and 192.80 for several sessions. Equal lows form at 191.00 — tested twice. This is obvious sell-side liquidity. Retail traders are shorting here with stops above 192.80.
②
The Fake Breakdown: Price breaks below 191.00 — retail breakout shorts enter, retail longs get stopped out. The 1H candle wicks to 190.72 but closes back above 191.00. This is the fakeout / liquidity grab. Retail shorts are now immediately trapped.
③
Why This is a Signal: The rejection wick below 191.00 shows the level held as support after liquidity was grabbed. The close back above equal lows + long wick = confirmation of sweep. The 4H context is bullish — this is the setup trigger.
④
CHoCH on 15M + OB Retest: On the 15M, price breaks a lower high (CHoCH). OB is at 191.00–191.25 (the candle body that formed the sweep origin). Price retraces into it — long entry triggered.
⑤
Target Logic: The target is the buy-side liquidity at 192.80 (top of range, where shorts' stops sit). The range high becomes the magnet. Stop below the sweep wick at 190.60. RR ≈ 4:1.
💡
Fakeout recognition pattern: Price breaks level → candle wicks beyond → candle closes BACK inside the range → this is the signal. The close back inside is everything. A true breakout would close and hold below. A fakeout closes back inside within the same or next 1–2 candles.

Invalidation Rules

Knowing when your trade idea is wrong is just as important as knowing when it's right. Invalidation is not a loss — it's information. A properly placed stop acts as your invalidation level.

Bullish Setup Invalidation

A bullish setup is invalidated when: Price closes below the sweep low (the wick extreme that started the move). This means the order block has been broken, the sweep low has been violated, and the institutional accumulation thesis is wrong. Exit immediately. Do not hope.
Valid Bullish: Sweep low (191.00) → CHoCH up → OB retest → holds → move to BSL ✓ Invalidated: Price returns and closes BELOW the sweep low (190.72 wick) → OB has failed, structure is now bearish → Close position, reassess, do NOT add to losers

Bearish Setup Invalidation

A bearish setup is invalidated when: Price closes above the sweep high. The bearish OB has been broken. The short thesis is wrong. The sweep was a genuine breakout, not a fakeout. Close the trade.

Partial Invalidation Signs (Warning Flags)

  • Price retests your OB multiple times without reacting — multiple retests weaken the OB
  • Price closes inside your OB but continues past the midpoint toward your stop — caution
  • A higher-timeframe BOS occurs against your trade direction
  • A news event fundamentally changes the macro picture

Common Mistakes

⛔
Trading every order block without structural context

An OB without a preceding sweep, CHoCH, or structural reason is just a candle. Order blocks are only meaningful when they form at key structural turning points following a liquidity event. Mark every OB but only trade the ones that meet all conditions.

⛔
Entering during the sweep, not after it

Many traders see price approaching SSL and immediately buy — only to get swept themselves. The entry is after the sweep wick has formed and the candle has closed back above the level. Patience here separates profitable traders from those who become the liquidity.

⛔
Confusing CHoCH and BOS — entering on CHoCH alone

CHoCH is a warning, not a confirmation. Many traders enter the moment CHoCH appears, before price has retraced to the OB. This results in poor entries with wide stops and no structure backing the trade. Wait for the OB pullback.

⛔
Ignoring the higher timeframe bias

Setting up a "perfect" bullish entry on the 15M chart while the daily chart is in a clear downtrend is a recipe for pain. Always align with the dominant HTF trend. Counter-trend trades require exceptional confluence and experience.

⛔
Moving stop-loss to avoid being stopped out

When price approaches your stop, widening the stop-loss defeats the entire purpose of invalidation. If price is at your stop, your thesis may be wrong. Let the stop do its job. Widening stops turns small losses into account-damaging ones.

⛔
Marking every swing as "buy-side" or "sell-side" liquidity

Not every swing high or low has meaningful liquidity. The most significant pools are at: equal highs/lows (double tops/bottoms), previous day/week highs and lows, major swing points that held for multiple sessions, and round numbers. Be selective — quality over quantity.

⛔
Expecting every liquidity sweep to reverse

Sometimes price sweeps a level and continues in the same direction — this is a genuine breakout with momentum. The difference: a fakeout has a long wick closing back inside. A real breakout closes and holds beyond the level with follow-through. Learn to distinguish.

⛔
Targeting the wrong liquidity pool

Your take-profit must be placed at the next meaningful liquidity pool in the direction of your trade — not just any level. If there is a major structural zone sitting between your entry and target, price may stall there. Map all liquidity before entering, and choose targets with clear path.

Practice Guide

Reading charts with this framework takes time and repetition. Here is a structured practice routine to accelerate learning — all on historical charts before ever risking real capital.

Week 1–2 — Structure Only
  • Open any forex pair on TradingView
  • Mark every swing HH, HL, LH, LL on the 4H chart
  • Identify every BOS and CHoCH you can find
  • Practice on 20 charts before moving on
  • Do not think about entries yet — just structure
Week 3–4 — Liquidity Mapping
  • On each chart, mark all equal highs (BSL) and equal lows (SSL)
  • Mark any double tops/bottoms as major liquidity zones
  • Watch replays: observe how price approaches and sweeps these zones
  • Note: does it fake out or break through? Study both cases
  • Keep a journal of what you observe
Week 5–6 — Order Blocks
  • After each BOS or CHoCH, identify the originating OB
  • Mark the OB zone (body of last opposing candle before impulse)
  • Watch replays: does price retrace into the OB? How does it react?
  • Mark valid OBs vs failed OBs — count the win rate over 50+ examples
Week 7–8 — Full Trade Simulation
  • Use TradingView's Replay feature or a demo account
  • Apply the 7-step framework to each setup
  • Write down: bias, liquidity, sweep, CHoCH, OB, entry, SL, TP
  • Track results: did the trade work? Why or why not?
  • Aim for 30+ simulated trades before going live

Recommended Chart Routine (Daily)

1. Open charts → Start on Daily TF — mark bias, major liquidity pools → Drop to 4H — identify structure (BOS / CHoCH), mark OBs → Drop to 1H/15M — find sweeps, refine OB, plan entry 2. Ask yourself these 5 questions for every setup: ① What is the HTF bias? ② What liquidity was just taken (or being approached)? ③ Has a CHoCH confirmed a shift? ④ Where is the order block I can enter from? ⑤ Where are my SL and TP, and is the RR at least 2:1? If you cannot answer all 5 clearly → no trade.
